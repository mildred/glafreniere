 THREE QUARKS, OR MAYBE JUST SIX ELECTRONS.

What are we looking for?

The goal is to find a "quark", a wave unit which can produce a proton or a neutron while three of them are joined. Because there are "up" and "down" quarks, they should be slightly different. So we are looking for three quarks, albeit one must realize that most observations on quarks appear quite fuzzy. We cannot see a quark, and even worst, it is impossible to observe one alone because it is highly unstable. Honestly, there could be no quarks at all, but rather a wave system which seems to contain three quarks.

  • The wave units should be made of only two electrons because they must be the simpler possible.

  • Electrons should be capable of attracting themselves in spite of their charge.

  • They should produce a gluonic field in order to justify this attraction effect  

  • They should still radiate a "colour charge": the four pi / 4 intermediate phases are obvious.

  • However the phase may vary from different points of view, as +2/3 and -1/3 opposite mean spins.

  • The three quarks units should be neutral because they can produce a neutron.

  • However it should be positive in its centre in order to accept a positron and become a proton.

  • It should show eight axis in order to explain the constant 8-electron external atomic shell. 

  • It should be capable of capturing one electron and produce an hydrogen atom.

  • Many protons should be capable of attracting themselves in spite of their charge.

  • This could be achieved by magnetic fields, hence the spin should be the same for more electrons.

  • Many protons should capture the same number of electrons in different periodic atomic shells.

  • Those shells should coincide with precise periodic zones in order to explain the spectral lines.

The wave mechanics indicates that an electron and a positron can produce two quark pairs. In the beginning of times, there was nothing else but electrons and positrons in equal quantities. This is explained in the page on a new Theory of evolution. So they must have attracted themselves and produced a lot of quark pairs. Just one quark is highly unstable, but two pairs can join an produce a much more stable unit. Finally a third quark can join and produce a neutron.

Fresnel's square of the amplitude law.

Augustin Fresnel discovered that the waves energy is worth the square of the amplitude. This law is one of the most well known of all, but most scientists still do not understand it well. For instance, the the laser beam produces an Airy disk which is postulated to be the result of the addition of billions of wavelets originating from the laser surface. However, the wavelets energy decreases according to the also well known square of the distance law. So it becomes difficult to understand why the laser energy does not significantly decrease until the distance has reached the "junction point".

The formula for this junction point is: L = D 2 / 2.44 l. For instance, the distance is 630 mm (about 2 feet for l = 0.00065) for a one millimeter aperture red laser. This happens because the waves emitted by two electrons will add their amplitude (1 + 1 = 2) while the energy will rather be the square of the total amplitude (2 times 2 = 4). 

The wave energy from 2 electrons will cancel while the phase is opposite, but it will be squared while the phase is the same. Because the waves distribution is sinusoidal, this would normally end with a simple additive result, but there is an unusual exception on the axis joining two electrons. The waves are always adding themselves there. They never cancel inside a space which is far wider then just one wavelength. This space is a "gluon", a gluonic field.
